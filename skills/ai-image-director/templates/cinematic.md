# Template — Cinematic Photography

Use this template for cinematic stills, atmospheric narrative scenes, environmental storytelling, mood-driven imagery, and any image that communicates narrative or emotion through visual language borrowed from film production.

---

## When to Use This Template

- Narrative stills for editorial, brand, or personal projects
- Environmental and atmospheric imagery with storytelling intent
- Cinematic portraits within a scene (person + strong environment)
- Concept and mood board generation for film or commercial pre-production
- Theatrical, dramatic, or high-concept visual content
- Night scenes, urban atmosphere, interior drama
- Landscape and environment as protagonist

---

## Cinematic vs. Editorial vs. Commercial

**Cinematic**: The image tells or implies a story. Atmosphere, tension, or narrative is as important as the subject.

**Editorial**: The image presents a subject — person, fashion, product — with craft and artistry. Subject is primary; atmosphere is secondary.

**Commercial**: The image communicates a brand message. Clarity and legibility are primary; atmosphere serves the message.

Use this template when story and atmosphere are not subordinate to subject clarity.

## Section 2: Cinematic Lighting

Cinematic lighting is motivated, intentional, and narrative. Every light source tells part of the story.

### Cinematic Lighting Principles

**Motivated lighting**: Light comes from sources that exist in the scene — windows, lamps, neon signs, fires, screens. Even if those sources are implied rather than visible.

**Mixed color temperatures**: Cinema frequently uses intentional temperature contrast. Cool exterior light from windows against warm interior tungsten. This contrast is narrative — it tells you something about inside vs. outside, present vs. past.

**High ratio / low key**: Deep shadow is information. In cinematic work, what is NOT seen is as important as what IS seen. Shadows create mystery, tension, and depth.

### Lighting Setups by Genre

**Noir**:
```
High contrast single-source lighting, hard shadows, 
venetian blind pattern casting shadow across subject, 
deep crushed shadows with no fill, 
practical motivated from one off-frame practical lamp, 
warm tungsten 2700K, cold blue from window at 90° to key creating opposite color temperature.
```

**Drama / prestige**:
```
Large soft source motivated by window on camera [DIRECTION], 
natural quality, 5000K neutral, 
very slight warm fill from opposite wall bounce, 
shadow side near-black but with edge detail retained, 
cinematographer quality, [DIRECTOR OF PHOTOGRAPHY REFERENCE].
```

**Thriller / suspense**:
```
Underexposed overall, one practical light source visible in frame, 
directional and harsh, deep pools of darkness in surrounding frame, 
subject lit from [DIRECTION] by single practical, 
face partially in shadow, eyes lit.
```

**Golden hour / emotional**:
```
Golden hour exterior light from low horizon, 3500K warm, 
long shadow cast by subject, 
backlit silhouette with face in warm fill from reflected sky, 
haze or atmospheric particles catching light.
```

**Blue hour / melancholy**:
```
Blue hour exterior, 9000K cool blue ambient, 
window light from interior casting orange-warm rectangle on exterior surface, 
color temperature contrast — cold outside, warm inside.
```

**Night exterior**:
```
Night scene, primary source from practical elements: 
[NEON SIGN — color and direction], [STREET LAMP — overhead, sodium vapor orange], 
[PHONE SCREEN GLOW — blue, from below], 
multiple competing color temperatures creating cinematic complexity.
```

## Section 3: Cinematic Camera Work

### Focal Length for Cinematic Work

| Focal Length | Cinematic Character |
|-------------|---------------------|
| 14–24mm | Wide, distorts, establishes environments, creates unease in dramatic contexts |
| 28–35mm | Street-level, intimate, personal distance, slight context |
| 40–50mm | Neutral perspective, closest to how the eye sees, realistic |
| 75–85mm | Portrait distance, separates subject from environment, classical drama |
| 135mm | Compression, flattening, observed from distance, surveillance feeling |
| 200mm+ | Extreme compression, long lens storytelling, separated subject |

### Cinematic Aspect Ratios

**Academy (1.375:1)**: Vintage, intimate, early sound era reference  
**Standard (1.5:1 / 3:2)**: Photography standard  
**Widescreen (1.78:1 / 16:9)**: Television and web cinematic  
**Anamorphic (2.39:1 / 2.4:1)**: Wide cinematic format, oval bokeh, horizontal lens flares

For a cinematic widescreen feel, add:
```
Anamorphic lens format, 2.39:1 aspect ratio, slight oval bokeh quality, 
characteristic horizontal lens flare from point light sources.
```

### Camera Position and Meaning

| Camera Height | Narrative Meaning |
|--------------|------------------|
| Eye level | Neutral, equal relationship with subject |
| Low angle | Subject powerful, elevated, dominant |
| High angle | Subject vulnerable, small, overwhelmed |
| Bird's eye | Detachment, god's-eye view, context reveals scale |
| Dutch angle (canted) | Unease, instability, wrongness, psychological tension |
| Ground level | Intimacy with world, subject observed from below |

## Section 4: Atmosphere and Environmental Effects

Atmosphere is visual information. Every atmospheric element communicates something.

### Atmospheric Vocabulary

**Rain**:
`wet surfaces, rain-slicked pavement, running water on glass, 
neon reflections in puddles, misty background, damp air quality, 
slight steam visible on warm surfaces`

**Fog / mist**:
`atmospheric haze, fog layer reducing background contrast, 
depth layers revealed by mist — near elements sharp, far elements fading, 
diffused light quality, no hard shadows`

**Smoke / haze**:
`[SMOKE TYPE — cigarette / atmospheric / industrial / fire] 
catching [LIGHT SOURCE] direction, volumetric light visible, 
particles suspended in air`

**Heat / summer**:
`heat shimmer visible in background, 
strong overhead light creating short harsh shadows, 
saturated color, dust in air, 
lens flare from direct sun`

**Snow**:
`snowfall in frame, caught by ambient light, 
muffled visual quality, high key white environment, 
[SNOW DEPTH AND COVERAGE], cold blue shadow quality`

**Golden dust / particles**:
`dust motes visible in shaft of light from [SOURCE], 
golden particle quality, slight magical realism`

## Section 5: Color and Grade for Cinematic Work

### Cinematic Grade References

**Standard modern drama**: `lifted shadow floor, slightly desaturated, cooler shadows, warmer highlights, compressed dynamic range, film-like`

**Neo-noir**: `deep crushed shadows, high contrast, teal shadows / orange highlights, high saturation in practical light sources`

**Period drama / vintage**: `warm golden overall grade, slightly faded, lifted blacks, film grain, slight green in shadows`

**Prestige / arthouse**: `clean grade, wide dynamic range, accurate color, film grain present, shadows have information`

**Science fiction / clinical**: `cool desaturated, near-monochromatic, blue or teal dominant, clinical precision`

**Horror / thriller**: `desaturated, green shadow cast, high contrast, underexposed overall, unnatural skin tones`

### Film Stock References

`Kodak Vision3 5219` — cinematic standard, warm, wide latitude  
`Kodak Vision3 250D` — balanced, clean, true color  
`Fujifilm Eterna` — cooler, flatter, sophisticated  
`Kodak 5294` — high contrast, vivid, dramatic  
`Film grain visible at 1:1, halation around highlight edges`
