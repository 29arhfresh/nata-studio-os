# Troubleshooting — Creative Director

Diagnosis and resolution for the most common creative failures. Organized by failure category.

---

## Brief Failures

### Problem: The objective keeps expanding mid-project

**Symptom**: New deliverables, new audiences, or new messages are added after the brief is signed off. Each addition requires revisiting earlier decisions.

**Root cause**: The objective was written as an activity ("create a campaign") rather than an outcome ("drive trial among 25-year-old urban women in Q3").

**Resolution**:
1. Rewrite the objective as a measurable outcome with a defined audience and timeframe.
2. Freeze the brief at Stage 1 sign-off. Any expansion after sign-off is a scope change and requires a new brief or a formal amendment.
3. Add a "Out of Scope" section to the brief listing what this project will not address.

---

### Problem: Tone descriptors are contradictory

**Symptom**: The brief lists tones like "bold" and "minimal", or "playful" and "luxury" — directions that produce visual conflicts at execution.

**Root cause**: Tone descriptors were generated without resolving the underlying brand tension.

**Resolution**:
1. Run a tone audit: for each pair of descriptors, ask "can one image be simultaneously [A] and [B]?" If no — they conflict.
2. Rank descriptors by priority. The top two define the primary register; the rest are modifiers.
3. Restate the tone as a position: "sophisticated at its core, with warmth at the edges" rather than listing both independently.

## Concept Failures

### Problem: The concept cannot be stated in one sentence

**Symptom**: Every attempt to articulate the concept requires qualifications, subclauses, and exceptions.

**Root cause**: The concept is actually two or three different ideas that have not been resolved into one.

**Resolution**:
1. Write all competing concept ideas on separate lines.
2. Identify the one idea that most directly connects the brand to the audience's desire.
3. Eliminate the others. They may become executional variations, but they are not the core concept.
4. If none can be chosen, return to the objective. A weak objective produces competing concepts.

---

### Problem: References are not translatable to the visual system

**Symptom**: The references named in the brief (e.g., "Stanley Kubrick" and "Instagram beige aesthetic") belong to incompatible visual registers.

**Root cause**: References were generated from general admiration rather than specific visual intent.

**Resolution**:
1. For each reference, extract the specific visual quality being borrowed: lighting setup, color relationship, composition approach, or texture vocabulary.
2. Discard references where the extracted quality conflicts with the approved tone.
3. Replace with references in the same category as the retained qualities.

## Visual System Failures

### Problem: The color palette is not ownable

**Symptom**: The approved palette is so generic (black, white, beige) that the deliverables could belong to any brand.

**Root cause**: Color strategy was derived from aesthetic preference rather than brand differentiation.

**Resolution**:
1. Audit the palette against the brand's top three competitors. Does it differentiate?
2. Introduce at least one color value that is specific to this brand — a particular tone of green, an unusual neutral, a distinctive warm-cool balance.
3. Adjust the accent color to carry the most differentiation burden. The primary and neutral can be conventional; the accent must be distinctive.

---

### Problem: Typography feels inconsistent across deliverables

**Symptom**: The same typeface looks different across hero images, social posts, and video lower thirds.

**Root cause**: Type was applied without scale relationships or tracking specifications.

**Resolution**:
1. Define a type scale ratio: primary is always 4× the secondary, secondary is always 2× the tertiary.
2. Define tracking: primary type at –20 to –30 tracking; secondary at 0; tertiary at +40 to +80.
3. Define leading: primary at 1.0–1.1× size; secondary at 1.4–1.6× size.
4. Apply these specifications in the art direction document for every deliverable.

## Art Direction Failures

### Problem: The art direction is being ignored by the production team

**Symptom**: Delivered work does not match the art direction document. The production team claims ambiguity.

**Root cause**: The art direction uses descriptive language ("soft", "clean", "editorial") rather than specifications.

**Resolution**:
1. Audit the art direction document for every vague adjective. Replace each with a specification:
   - "Soft" → "f/2.8, single diffused source, no fill"
   - "Clean" → "white background, zero props, no shadow drama"
   - "Editorial" → "Negative space: subject occupies 25% of frame; remaining 75% is solid background"
2. Add a "Do Not" section: three explicit visual patterns to avoid.
3. Include a minimum of two reference images per deliverable in the art direction brief.

## Quality Score Failures

### Problem: All dimensions score similarly — the review does not identify what to fix

**Symptom**: Every dimension scores 6–7. The weighted total passes, but the work is average across the board.

**Root cause**: Scoring is calibrated too generously or too conservatively.

**Resolution**:
1. Recalibrate using the anchor descriptions: a score of 10 means the deliverable is the best example of that dimension you have seen in this category. A score of 5 means it is average — present but not notable.
2. Force rank the five dimensions for this specific deliverable. The lowest-ranked dimension should score lowest.
3. Compare against a known benchmark (a reference campaign) rather than scoring in isolation.

---

### Problem: The revision directive is not being acted on

**Symptom**: The same issue reappears after a revision cycle. The score does not improve.

**Root cause**: The revision directive described the problem but not the specific fix.

**Resolution**:
1. Rewrite the directive in this format: "[Who] will [do what specific action] by [when] to produce [what specific result]."
   - Example: "The retoucher will remove the warm color grade and apply the approved LUT file (nova-cc-v2.cube) to the hero image by Thursday EOD."
2. Schedule a check-in at the midpoint of the revision cycle — do not wait for the completed revision to discover it was interpreted incorrectly.

## Cross-Campaign Consistency Failures

### Problem: Deliverables from the same campaign feel like different brands

**Symptom**: The hero image, the social reels, and the copy line do not feel like they belong together.

**Root cause**: Each deliverable was produced by a different team member with independent interpretation of the brief, without a shared visual system reference.

**Resolution**:
1. Run the cross-campaign consistency audit from `CHECKLIST.md` before any deliverable is approved.
2. Identify the one element that most clearly signals brand identity in the approved deliverables (usually the color palette) and enforce it as the consistency anchor.
3. For future campaigns, distribute the approved visual system as a one-page reference sheet to every production team member before work begins.
